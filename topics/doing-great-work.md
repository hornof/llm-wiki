## What This Is

A synthesis of career and research philosophy relevant to anyone trying to produce first-class technical work — specifically in AI engineering, where the gap between good and great output is wide and widening. Primary source: [[richard-hamming]]'s 1986 talk "You and Your Research." Secondary lens: the wiki itself is an instance of Karpathy's [[llm-wiki-pattern]], which is itself an application of some of these principles (compounding knowledge, building on your own prior work).

## Core Principles

### 1. Work on Important Problems
An "important problem" is one where you have a reasonable line of attack — not just a consequential goal. Time travel is consequential; it is not important (no attack exists). The question to ask: "What are the most important problems in my field, and which ones can I actually make progress on?"

Hamming kept 10–20 such problems in mind at all times. When a new idea appeared, he could immediately map it to a problem and act.

### 2. Knowledge Compounds
"Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest." An extra hour of focused thinking per day, sustained over years, dramatically outperforms talent alone. The implication: consistency and sustained engagement matter more than bursts.

This is structural justification for a knowledge base like this wiki — the earlier you build the compounding system, the longer it runs.

### 3. Emotional Commitment Drives the Subconscious
Deep commitment to a problem means the subconscious works on it continuously. Hamming: "Keep your subconscious starved so it has to work on your problem." Shallow engagement produces shallow results.

### 4. Tolerate Ambiguity
Great scientists believe their theory enough to proceed, doubt it enough to catch its errors. Too much certainty = blind spots; too much doubt = paralysis. Darwin forced himself to record contradictory evidence explicitly, knowing it would otherwise disappear from memory.

### 5. Reframe Constraints as Assets
Hamming had no programmers, so he invented compilers. Grace Hopper, same situation. "What appeared at first to me as a defect forced me into automatic programming very early." The pattern recurs: constraint → forced creativity → breakthrough.

### 6. Sell Your Work
Communication is not optional overhead. Writing, formal talks, and informal hallway conversations are how ideas propagate and get credit attached. "It ain't what you do, it's the way that you do it." At least 50% of effort should go to presentation.

### 7. Open Doors
Working with the door open creates interruptions but also delivers clues about what matters. The closed-door worker produces more short-term but drifts from important problems over time. Correlated with long-run impact.

### 8. Change Fields Periodically
Originality in a field gets used up. Hamming recommended deliberately shifting every ~7 years into an adjacent area — starting over as a novice, replanting the acorns. Shannon's failure to do this after information theory effectively ended his productive scientific career.

### 9. Don't Let Recognition Sterilize You
Early fame narrows the problem space people will attempt. "Now he could only work on great problems." The acorn-planting stops; the big oaks never grow. The antidote: continue to take small, generative bets even after success.

## Applied to AI Engineering

- The "important problems" question translates directly: what problems in AI engineering have a tractable line of attack right now? (Evals, agent reliability, cost efficiency, context management — more tractable than AGI alignment or novel architectures.)
- The compound interest principle is why this wiki exists: each ingest adds marginal value; the structure multiplies it.
- The "sell your work" principle applies to writing, talks, and GitHub presence — the career artifacts that create visibility.
- Field rotation maps well to a career transition: returning to hands-on work after a VP/CTO period is exactly Hamming's prescription — reset, replant.
