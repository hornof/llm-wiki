## Who They Are

Richard Wesley Hamming (1915–1998) was a mathematician at Bell Labs from 1946 to 1976. Inventor of Hamming codes (error-correcting codes fundamental to all digital communication), the Hamming window (signal processing), and Hamming distance. Turing Award winner (1968). He shared offices and lunch tables with Shannon, Tukey, and others at Bell Labs during one of the most productive periods in the history of applied science. Later became a professor at the Naval Postgraduate School.

Relevant to this wiki not for AI research per se, but for his systematic thinking about what makes scientific careers produce great results — directly applicable to an AI engineering career transition.

## Their Current Focus

Deceased (1998). Primary legacy: error-correcting codes and the essay/talk "You and Your Research" (1986), which has become one of the most-circulated pieces of career philosophy in science and engineering.

## Notable Takes

- **Work on important problems, not safe ones**: "The average scientist spends almost all his time working on problems which he believes will not be important." The key is having a reasonable line of attack, not just a consequential outcome.
- **Compound interest on effort**: An extra hour of thinking per day, sustained over a career, dramatically outpaces talent advantages.
- **Change your field every ~7 years**: Shannon's failure to do this after information theory effectively ended his scientific career. You run out of original ideas in a field; move nearby and start over as a novice.
- **Sell your work**: Great science that isn't communicated effectively doesn't count. Presentation is not vanity — it's half the job.
- **Open door principle**: "I notice a pretty good correlation between those who work with the doors open and those who ultimately do important things."
- **UNIX aside**: Hamming claims that giving Bell Labs researchers a small machine (instead of a big shared one) is what forced the conditions that produced UNIX.
