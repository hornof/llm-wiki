## Summary
Simon Willison reports that as coding agents become more reliable, he no longer reviews every line of AI-generated code, even in production work — the supposed firewall between [[vibe-coding]] (casual, unreviewed) and [[agentic-engineering]] (professional, reviewed) is collapsing in his own practice. He frames this as uncomfortable rather than triumphant: he wants higher-quality output, not just less-checked output.

## Key Claims / Takeaways
- **Convergence claim** (verbatim): "The problem is that as the coding agents get more reliable, I'm not reviewing every line of code that they write anymore, even for my production level stuff."
- **Productivity vs. trust tradeoff** (verbatim): "I can knock out a git repository with a hundred commits and a beautiful readme and comprehensive tests... in half an hour! It looks identical to those projects that have had a great deal of care."
- **Quality stake** (verbatim): "I want to build _higher_ quality stuff faster. I want everything I'm building to be better in every way than it was before."
- **Tension with Karpathy's two-mode framing**: Karpathy's distinction in [[karpathy-vibe-coding-agentic-engineering]] presents vibe coding (floor-raising) and agentic engineering (ceiling-raising) as complementary, distinguished by who reviews what. Willison's report is that in practice, even ceiling-side practitioners are slipping into floor-side trust as the agents get more reliable. This is *not* a claim that the two modes are the same; it is a report that the practitioner discipline keeping them apart is harder to maintain.
- **Practitioner credibility**: Willison is a long-tracked, high-signal voice (creator of Datasette, co-creator of Django, prolific blogger on LLM tooling). First Willison primary source captured in this wiki.
- **Named references in the piece**: Joseph Ruscio (Heavybit's *High Leverage* podcast); Jenny Wen (Anthropic design leader); Matthew Yglesias (political commentator); [[claude-code]].
