## Summary

Transcript of Richard Hamming's 1986 talk at Bellcore. Hamming — Bell Labs mathematician, inventor of error-correcting codes and the Hamming window — spent decades observing what separated great scientists from merely good ones. The talk synthesizes those observations into practical guidance on doing first-class research. One of the most-cited essays on research productivity and career philosophy in science and engineering.

## Key Claims / Takeaways

- **Work on important problems**: "If you do not work on an important problem, it's unlikely you'll do important work." Important ≠ prestigious — it means a problem where you have a reasonable line of attack, not just a consequential goal.
- **Maintain a list**: Great scientists carry 10–20 important problems in mind. When a new idea appears, they immediately recognize which problem it bears on and drop everything.
- **Luck favors the prepared mind** (Pasteur): Luck is real but partial. Prepared minds encounter the same opportunities and act on them.
- **Knowledge compounds**: "Given two people with the same ability, the one who gets in one more hour of thinking per day will be tremendously more productive over a lifetime."
- **Tolerate ambiguity**: Great scientists believe their theory enough to proceed, doubt it enough to notice its flaws. Darwin forced himself to write down every piece of contradictory evidence.
- **Emotional commitment drives the subconscious**: Deep commitment starves the subconscious of distractions, forcing it to work on your problem while you sleep.
- **Open doors matter**: Closed-door workers produce more day-to-day but lose track of what problems matter. Open doors bring serendipitous clues.
- **Sell your work**: Communication (writing, formal talks, informal hallway talks) is not optional. "It ain't what you do, it's the way that you do it."
- **Reframe defects as assets**: Hamming's lack of programmers forced him into automatic programming (compilers). Constraints generate originality.
- **Change fields every ~7 years**: Prevents staleness and forces replanting of acorns. Shannon ruined his career by not doing this after information theory.
- **Don't let fame sterilize you**: Early recognition leads scientists to attempt only "great" problems, killing the acorn-planting that creates future oaks.
- **Personality defects cost**: Ego assertion, anger, fighting the system — each extracts a steady tax on productive output over a career.
- **"Great Thoughts Time"**: Hamming dedicated Friday lunch to thinking only about big-picture questions in his field.
