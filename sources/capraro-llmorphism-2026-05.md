## Summary

arXiv preprint (May 6 2026, 16 pages, cs.CY) by **Valerio Capraro** introducing the term **LLMorphism**: *"the biased belief that human cognition works like a large language model."* As LLMs produce increasingly human-like language, Capraro argues people are starting to infer the inverse — that human thinking *operates* like an LLM. The paper names the cognitive shortcuts that drive the misattribution (analogical transfer, metaphorical availability) and traces consequences across work, education, accountability, healthcare, interpersonal communication, creative process, and human dignity. Sociological/conceptual paper, not empirical.

## Key Claims / Takeaways

### The coinage
**LLMorphism** = the bias of inferring that *because LLMs produce human-like outputs*, humans *must produce outputs the way LLMs do*. The term sits in the same family as anthropomorphism but inverted — anthropomorphism is attributing human qualities to non-humans; LLMorphism is attributing LLM qualities to humans.

### Two driver mechanisms
1. **Analogical transfer** — when something behaves like X in one dimension, observers transfer X's mechanism to it. Because LLMs produce fluent text, observers transfer the LLM mechanism (statistical next-token prediction) onto the human production of fluent text.
2. **Metaphorical availability** — LLM terminology becomes the default vocabulary for cognition (memory as "context window," thinking as "token generation"). Once the metaphor is dominant, it shapes what claims feel intuitive — even where the underlying mechanism doesn't apply.

### Core argument
Similarity in *output* does not imply identity of *mechanism*. Two systems can produce indistinguishable text via radically different processes. Treating human cognition as operationally LLM-like is a category error — but a culturally seductive one.

### The most quotable line
> "The issue is not only whether we are attributing too much mind to machines, but also whether we are beginning to attribute too little mind to humans."

This frames LLMorphism as the **flip-side risk to anthropomorphism**: the dominant ethics conversation has worried about ascribing too much mind to AI; this paper argues we should equally worry about ascribing too *little* mind to ourselves once the LLM frame becomes the cultural default.

### Domains affected
The paper traces implications across:
- **Work** — productivity / capability framing
- **Education** — what learning *is* (rote token-prediction vs. understanding)
- **Accountability** — moral responsibility under "we're all just predicting"
- **Healthcare** — therapeutic relationships if cognition is reduced to language modeling
- **Interpersonal communication** — empathy and theory-of-mind
- **Creative processes** — originality framing
- **Human dignity** — the most load-bearing domain; if humans are "just LLMs," the moral premium for human-ness erodes

## Why This Matters for the Wiki

- The wiki is built around the [[llm-wiki-pattern]] — *Obsidian is the IDE; the LLM is the programmer; the wiki is the codebase*. That metaphor is operationally useful and philosophically coherent. **It is also the kind of metaphor LLMorphism warns about** — once the wiki becomes "the codebase" and the LLM becomes "the programmer," it's easy to think of the human curator as a *compiler* rather than the source of intent. The paper is a useful prophylactic against the slide from working metaphor to ontological claim.
- Useful frame for evaluating how AI engineering vocabulary ("attention," "memory," "context window") gets adopted in non-AI human-cognition discussions in the next 6-12 months.

## Caveats

- **Sociological/conceptual paper, not empirical.** No measurements of how widespread LLMorphism is, no validated detection methodology. The paper proposes the concept; downstream work would need to test it.
- **Single-author** (Capraro). No prior sustained signal in this wiki — track for whether other practitioners pick up the term.
- The term "LLMorphism" itself may not survive — a community of LLM researchers may coin a better one, or absorb the idea into existing critiques without adopting the label. Track adoption before treating the term as vocabulary.
