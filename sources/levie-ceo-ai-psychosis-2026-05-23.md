## Summary

[[aaron-levie|Aaron Levie]] X post (2026-05-23, 2.7M followers): *"CEOs are uniquely prone to AI psychosis because they're sufficiently distant from the last mile of work that still has to happen to generate most value with AI. So when they play with AI, they see the happy path results, often not considering the next 10 or 20 things that have to happen to get sustainable results from agents."* The post is the named-anchor of the **CEO-distance-from-last-mile** thesis later carried into TechCrunch (Julie Bort, 2026-05-27) and adjacent practitioner posts. Levie's own positioning: not an AI critic — angel investor, *"Headless software is the future"*, mostly AI-positive — which makes the post a *within-camp* critique rather than a contrarian-skeptic take.

## Key Claims / Takeaways

### The thesis (verbatim structure)

- **Mechanism**: CEOs are *"sufficiently distant from the last mile"* of value-creation work.
- **Failure mode**: they see happy-path prototype results and don't *"consider the next 10 or 20 things that have to happen to get sustainable results from agents."*
- **Two examples Levie names**:
  - *"Look I made this awesome product prototype"* — but no code review, no production-fix loop.
  - *"Look I generated a contract"* — but no term verification, no wiring to past contracts.
- **Prescription**: CEOs should use AI *"a *ton*"* themselves to develop calibrated intuition for *"both the upside and the real work that goes into them."*

### Within-camp positioning (why this matters)

- Levie is an angel investor in AI startups, runs an AI-native-positioning company ([[box|Box]]), and posts mostly AI-positivity. The post is **not** a skeptic call — it's a *calibration call from inside the AI-bull camp.* Less easily dismissed than an Ed-Zitron-shaped external critique.
- Comment-thread amplification by Michal Malewicz: *"CEOs are the most delusional about AI. Detached from reality."* — sharpens the framing without Levie's within-camp hedging.

### Anshu Sharma's executive-assistant test ("the true Turing test")

- Comment from Anshu Sharma (@anshublog): *"New rule: any ceo who claims work can be fully done by ai needs to immediately let go of their executive assistant. Oh so you're telling me it can do the job of a software engineer that builds schedulers but not that of a scheduler?"*
- Levie's reply: *"The true Turing test."*
- **Why it matters**: surfaces a concrete operational falsifier — if a CEO believes AI can do an SWE's job but personally relies on a human EA for scheduling, the belief is unfalsified-by-personal-practice. Useful tool for cutting through CEO AI claims.

### Alexander Wulff: possibility-layer vs consequences-layer

- *"CEOs often interact with AI at the 'possibility layer' while operators interact with it at the 'consequences layer.' Both perspectives matter, but they feel very different once real customers, edge cases, and production accountability enter the picture."*
- **Useful taxonomy** for the broader framing — clean distinction between the two layers that the rest of the thread orbits around.

### Matt Hire's 4-hour-demo / 3-week-implementation anecdote

- *"Sat in a boardroom last month where the CEO demoed an AI workflow he built over the weekend. Took him 4 hours. His team spent the next 3 weeks figuring out how to make it work with their actual data, their actual compliance requirements, and 14 edge cases the demo never hit."*
- **Concrete operational instance** of the Levie thesis: 4-hour weekend prototype → 3-week production hardening at 14× wall-clock multiplier. Single anecdote, not a dataset, but illustrative.

### Counter-take (Praveen Koka)

- *"Cloud and big data CEOs also saw the happy path results but missed all the hard work. 'Uniquely' AI?"*
- **The thesis isn't AI-unique** — it's the recurring shape of any new-tech executive-vs-operator perception gap. Pairs with [[deedydas-sf-ai-wealth-divide-2026-05-15]] Hot Aisle counter-take: *"None of this is new."*
