## Key Claims / Takeaways

### Jeffries's framing — "compute is NOT the only RSI bottleneck"

The Anthropic Institute publication states:

> *"[If] AI systems themselves become capable of full recursive self-improvement... In this world, the pace of progress in AI development becomes determined entirely by the availability of compute (or the speed of discovering various efficiencies in algorithmic training or inference) for AI systems."*

Jeffries's critique:

> *"Compute is absolutely NOT the only limiting factor in recursive self-improvement and not even the most important one. They are two more: 1) Time, 2) Multiplicity."*

### The Time bottleneck

> *"Time is how long it takes to get an answer."*

**Examples**:
- *"Will my wife like this surprise present?"* — *"Took me many years of trying and judging her responses."*
- *"'Did I make money in business?' can't be answered in a short time line. There is no way to know the answer faster, and short term success doesn't predict long term."*
- *"'Will this drug cause bad side effects twenty years from now?' That can only be answered in twenty years. No amount of compute changes that."*
- *"'Will this building fall down faster than this one if I build it a different way?' You can run basic physics and math rules to help you heuristically figure it out, but only time gives you the true answer."*

### The Multiplicity bottleneck

> *"Multiplicity is when there is no right or wrong answers but only shades of gray with right(ish) answers and wrong(ish)."*

**Examples**:
- *"You cannot answer the question 'is this a good article?' or 'do I write well?' because that is multiplicity, shades of gray that are hard to judge."*
- *"Humans judge this by self-awareness and feedback from others. AI might be able to approximate the second but only if it develops more of the first (harder)."*

### Why AI got good at code, games, driving

> *"AI got good at code and games because they have great feedback loops and tight timelines. If the code works or does not, you know pretty quickly."*

> *"It [got] good at driving for the same reasons. Don't die or drive off the road or hit someone are achievable (though difficult) goals with clear, fast feedback."*

**Pattern**: tight feedback loops + clear ground truth = AI-capability-rapid-improvement domains. The reverse (slow feedback + ambiguous ground truth) = bottleneck domains. **First wiki-captured Jeffries-canonical AI-capability-domain-typology** mapping feedback-loop-tightness × ground-truth-clarity → AI-capability-improvement-rate.

### The conclusion

> *"The recursive self-improvement loop doesn't hit a wall because of compute. It hits a wall because of reality."*

> *"Reality is slow, messy, ambiguous, and full of questions that only time and lived experience can answer."*

> *"Compute is the bottleneck that engineers see because it's the one they can measure. Time and multiplicity are the bottlenecks that the real world imposes and no amount of silicon can brute force past them."*

### The hopeful framing

> *"AI is changing the world already. It will get better and better. But the road to better is long and winding, not a vertical line to godhood. And that should make you more hopeful, not less."*

### LeCun's "exponential" framing clarification

In the same thread, LeCun replies to Alex Grenier's *"Excavators are exponentially faster than shovels"* framing:

> *"You can't say 'A is exponentially &lt;x&gt;er than B'. I know lot of people say things like that, but it makes no sense. You could say that A and B are on a growth curve that appears (locally) exponential. But you need more than two points on the curve to identify it as exponential."*
