## Summary

Dwarkesh Patel essay (mid-May 2026) arguing that the popular framing of AGI risk via *"goal-achievement across domains"* is **definitionally broken** — under that definition, Stalin and Genghis Khan would be the most intelligent humans in history, which is *"not a cute gotcha but a definitional collapse that breaks every serious conversation about AGI risk."* Brief synopses captured across the 2026-05-17 and 2026-05-18 Daily Briefs; primary article was not deeply fetched, so verbatim quotes are minimal. Captured here as the structural argument with verification-pending depth.

## Key Claims / Takeaways

### The Stalin example as definitional check

- *"Intelligence ≠ power. Stalin achieved goals across domains — so did Genghis Khan. If that's your definition of intelligence, you've defined the word into uselessness and missed the actual alignment problem."* (2026-05-18 brief paraphrase.)
- The argument cuts in both directions:
  - **For AGI risk**: conflating intelligence with goal-achievement makes existential-risk arguments incoherent because it lets you predicate "superintelligence" on any sufficiently-effective-at-acquiring-resources system, regardless of whether the system actually reasons.
  - **For AGI capability**: same conflation muddies what "intelligence" means in safety contexts — Stalin's effectiveness was largely about power-and-coercion, not cognitive primitives like search, learning, planning, abstract reasoning.

### Structural framing

- *"Conflating intelligence with goal-achievement is how you end up saying Stalin was the smartest human ever."* (2026-05-18 brief framing.)
- Dwarkesh's broader thesis (recurring across his recent writing): **definitional precision matters more than benchmark performance** for the AGI conversation. If "intelligence" and "power" are not disambiguated, neither risk framing nor capability framing can be made coherent.

## Caveats

- **Primary article not fetched.** Only the brief-driven synopses + Daily Brief draft outlines are captured. Specific arguments beyond the Stalin example, Dwarkesh's stance on the implications for capability research, and any named alternative framings are not captured. Worth a deep-read pass if the essay is referenced in future ingests.
- **Brief framing may not match Dwarkesh's actual emphasis.** The two Daily Briefs' Drafts 1 and 2 differ slightly in framing; Dwarkesh's own emphasis may be different from either.
- **The Stalin example is rhetorical, not philosophical proof.** It's a useful intuition pump but doesn't actually establish that goal-achievement-across-domains is the wrong definition of intelligence — it just shows that the definition has counterintuitive consequences. The deeper philosophical move would be defining intelligence in terms of cognitive primitives (search, learning, abstraction, planning) rather than outcomes.
